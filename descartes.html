Disproving Descartes’ Divine Definition Divination

In René Descartes’ Meditations on First Philosophy, Descartes seeks to prove both God’s existence and that his ‘clear and distinct’ perceptions—many of which he uses to support his argument for God’s existence—must be true as a result. His argument for God’s existence centers around his conviction in God’s essence, the definition of God’s nature. However, Descartes does not know that his definition of God defines God, and accordingly, Descartes cannot prove the existence of the divine he thinks exists without fully knowing exactly what the divine is.

Descartes defines God’s essence to be “infinite substance, independent, supremely intelligent, and supremely powerful – the thing from which I and everything else get our existence” (Descartes 145). Furthermore, he claims that “I can’t conceive of there being two or more Gods,” which he supports earlier in his argument by deriving God’s existence for the existence of one “archetype,” defined as the cause, or source, of all ideas (Descartes 152, 144). He claims that it is impossible to acquire the idea of God through his senses, and thus his image of God must be from God himself, arguing that “I who am finite would not have the idea of infinite substance in me unless it came from a substance that really was infinite” (Descartes 145). Finally, Descartes contends that “God is the only thing that I can think of whose existence belongs to its essence,” in other words stating that the definition of God and His existence are intrinsically linked, as are the thought of a mountain and that of a valley (Descartes 152). From the assertion that God’s essence cannot be separated from His existence, Descartes argues that to merely think of God’s essence must prove God’s existence. He justifies these definitions of God’s essence through a logically suspect argument where he argues that his convictions relating to God’s essence must be true because the “light of nature” revealed them to him.

However, it is not clear that Descartes knows or can know enough of God’s essence to prove God’s existence. Descartes’ argument for his knowledge of God’s essence contains a paradox that stems from his first construction of the differences between presentational and formal ideas. In his explanation, he gives the example of having, “two different ideas of the sun,” namely: one he sees through his senses, and one he inferences through calculations (Descartes 143). His full presentational idea of the sun then constitutes both his visual perception and his astronomical calculations. Both parts get some form of reality from their cause, which in this case is the sun’s formal reality. A logical continuation of this argument is that if one has infinite perspectives of something, their presentational understanding of that object is the formal reality that object contains. The paradox lies here: Descartes defines the essence of God as that of a “supremely intelligent” being (Descartes 145). We can assume intelligence – which Descartes fails to fully explain in his Meditations – comes in part from understanding, as he uses intellect and understanding interchangeably in Meditation II. Understanding arises from the ability to grasp presentational reality as close to formal reality as possible; to recognize true form. Thus, the supreme intelligence attributed to God necessitates the supreme ability to perceive the true form and essence of everything. If God is supremely intelligent and has infinite substance as Descartes states, God must, by definition, be the only being that can grasp God’s true form and essence. This presumably means that Descartes, in claiming to see God’s formal reality as God sees it, is either contradicting himself and his definition or claiming to be supremely intelligent.

Descartes, however, has admitted that he is not supremely intelligent. He’s been wrong before, as he notes that “I used to believe myself to grasp clearly but did not really grasp at all” (Descartes 142). He even admits that “the gradual increase in my knowledge shows that I am imperfect, not that I am perfect,” as the act of increasing his knowledge concedes that gaps can exist in his knowledge (Descartes 146). Descartes cannot be deceived about his imperfection, as it is impossible to be both perfect and deceived. Because he acknowledges his lack supreme intelligence, Descartes is clearly not supremely intelligent and thus cannot grasp God’s formal reality, putting his understanding of God’s essence in question.

Even if, through valid reasoning, all the qualities Descartes stated could formally be attributed to God, Descartes still would not know that his understanding of God defines God, for the burden of knowledge for singular essence is infinite. In his attempt to prove God’s existence, Descartes compares his definition of God’s essence to the definition of a triangle’s essence. However, there is a key difference: The definition of a triangle was created to define all pre-existing triangles, and it thus can exist alongside other attributes a triangle may have. However, since God is singular (by Descartes’ definition), any definition of God’s essence must fully define Him. This is because a definition of a singular idea must be absent of omission, as it must completely depict the idea, because it must be the idea. Descartes is unable to prove that the qualities he gives God are the only true qualities of God in the same way that I cannot know the essence of a forest by closely inspecting a few square meters or even spending an entire lifetime devoted to studying the forest. Therefore, even if Descartes can soundly attribute qualities to God, his understanding remains limited by the unknown, and he thus fails to define God.

As Descartes cannot grasp God’s essence, it’s clear that his idea of God is merely presentational, acquired and produced from presentational ideas with finite substance such as God’s works. While Descartes paints his idea of God as innate, saying, “The more I consider these attributes, the less it seems like they could have come from me alone,” it is very much possible to conceive of God without an innate idea of Him (Descartes 145). Indeed, one presentational idea with more perfections than any one of its causes, such as God, can be created from a combination of many– or in this case all – of the existing causes perceived. Thomas Hobbes provides a framework for this in his objections to the Meditations, providing possible causes for each attribute that Descartes ascribes to God. Substance comes, “not from an idea but from reasoning,” infinite nature from the idea of “boundaries and limits,” independence from lack of origin and limits as well, and “supremely powerful” from the continued state of being (Bennett 49, Descartes 145). Notably, Hobbes, asks, “What […] is the idea through which Descartes understands the operation of God’s understanding?”, rejecting Descartes’ grounds for defining or even attributing supreme intelligence to God (Bennet 49). Of course, the idea of supreme intelligence, if it is part of God’s essence, can be produced through a combination of our capacity to reason and the aforementioned notion of infinite nature. Thus, all the qualities Descartes attributes to God can be acquired as presentational reality from existing causes.

Descartes has failed to prove that his definition of God’s essence is true or complete, instead relying on his clear & distinct thoughts in a seemingly circular manner to support this assertion, all while ignoring the paradoxical nature of his purported knowledge. Descartes has failed to prove that he has anything more than a presentational understanding of God’s essence and therefore cannot justify God’s existence in the form that he envisions.
