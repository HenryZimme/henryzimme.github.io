About

I am a junior at Phillips Academy Andover, working at the intersection of asteroseismology, time-domain astronomy, and the question of how stars remember their pasts.

My research centers on binary Cepheid variables: whether the pulsation signatures inside stars can tell us they were once two bodies that became one. I build custom analysis pipelines in Python, collaborate with professional astrophysicists, and work with archival photometry from the OGLE, MACHO, and VELOCE surveys.

Outside astronomy, I captain the cross-country team, play jazz guitar, and founded RenewBlue, a campus sustainability initiative. I am drawn to problems that require patience, precision, and a willingness to tear down a model when the data demands it.

Research
Primary Research — In preparation for ApJ Letters

First Asteroseismic Evidence of Merger Origin in a Binary Cepheid

I analyzed six years of OGLE photometry for OGLE-LMC-CEP-1347, a double-overtone binary Cepheid in the Large Magellanic Cloud with the shortest known orbital period in its class (~59 days). Using a custom prewhitening and Light-Time Travel Effect correction pipeline, I recovered a non-radial frequency triplet with spacing Δf ≈ 0.0074 c/d (R² = 0.99) — the signature of rotation-induced mode splitting. The implied rotation period of ~135 days is substantially slower than the orbital period in an already-circularized system. Since tidal theory predicts synchronization precedes circularization by orders of magnitude, finding asynchronous rotation in a circular orbit indicates the Cepheid's spin was recently disrupted by a merger. Mode switching among four pulsation modes across the observing baseline provides additional evidence of a transient post-merger thermal state. This constitutes what appears to be the first asteroseismic confirmation of merger origin in a binary Cepheid.

Ongoing — Asteroid Rotation Periods

Synodic Period Determination for 7605 Cindygraber

I am determining the first confirmed synodic rotation period for main-belt asteroid 7605 Cindygraber. I trained machine learning models on the ALCDEF Light Curve Database to predict period convergence requirements, wrote a formal observing proposal supported by this analysis, and am currently coordinating multi-site photometric observations across Chile, Australia, and the Canary Islands. I am also extracting spectra from diffraction grating images of the asteroid to constrain its taxonomic classification.

Open Source Tool

Multi-Site Asteroid Observing Scheduler

A Python tool that identifies optimal multi-site observing windows for asteroid targets by querying MPC ephemerides, computing per-site astronomical twilight dynamically, and modeling lunar sky brightness using the Krisciunas-Schaefer model. Built to solve a practical coordination problem in my own research; now used to schedule observations across three hemispheres for 7605 Cindygraber.
