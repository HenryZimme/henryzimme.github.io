Moral Autonomy and the Due North: Agency in Jane Eyre

By Henry Zimmerman

Throughout Charlotte Brontë’s Jane Eyre, men with power over Jane assert their moralities over her actions, creating an untenable contrast with her ethical will. To resolve these tensions, Jane must either change her moral framework or her status quo. Through her conflicts with Mr. Brocklehurst, John Reed, Mr. Rochester, and St. John Rivers, Jane is convinced of the necessity of her freedom from the imposition of the incompatible morals of others, leading her to prioritize her independence as a means of morality.

Lacking the power to change her material situation during her childhood, Jane responds to these conflicts by changing her morality to prioritize freedom from the oppressive forces she encounters. During her time at Gateshead, Jane’s morality is first shown when John Reed attacks her for reading. She derives her powerful resistance to John Reed's abuse from the moral lessons she found in the very historical accounts she is bashed for reading, forming opinions of "Roman emperors" and "slave-drivers" to articulate her own reality.

"Wicked and cruel boy! I said. You are like a murderer—you are like a slave-driver—you are like the Roman emperors!"

As Jane matures, the threats to her autonomy become more "magnetic" and sophisticated. While Mr. Rochester attempts to pull Jane into a life that would compromise her integrity, it is St. John Rivers who presents the ultimate challenge. St. John’s religious fervor acts as a gravitational force, attempting to pull Jane’s moral needle toward his own "universalist" dogma. He doesn't just want Jane; he wants to enlist her as a tool for his own narrow definition of divinity.

Jane’s breakthrough occurs when she recognizes that St. John’s "magnetism" is a flawed human construct. She declares her need for a path shown by Heaven alone, rendering her morality independent of the flawed humans who surround her. Upon Jane's break from St. John's grasp, she declares, "It was my time to assume ascendency. My powers were in play, and in force." Jane fully uncouples her moral compass from St. John's magnetism, pointing herself back to the due North of God.

Brontë uses Jane's fight for moral and religious autonomy as an analogue for the larger fight against a prescriptivism that denies agency to women. Jane's example displays both the challenges of 19th-century English society and the reductionist nature of universalist religious dogma. Through Jane Eyre, Brontë presents a path forward for women that calls for them to actively use the intertwined nature of their agency and ethics to achieve moral and personal ascendancy.
