# henryzimmerman.net

Source for my personal research and writing portfolio, live at [henryzimmerman.net](https://henryzimmerman.net).

I'm a student at Phillips Academy Andover working in time-domain astronomy. My research follows a single question: how much can a ground-based observation actually tell us, and how do we understand, reach, and surpass those limits? 

At the moment, I'm working on two projects in different subfields (survey design and Cepheid evolution), but they both came out of the same analysis (detailed in the [methods appendix](https://henryzimmerman.net/1347_methods)). After asking about binary evolution in Cepheids at AAS 247, I analyzed six years of archival photometry for [OGLE-LMC-CEP-1347](https://henryzimmerman.net/cep1347), a binary Cepheid in the Large Magellanic Cloud that's hypothesized to be a merger remnant. I found a convincing 5-sigma signal that looked like a tidally-split non-radial pulsation mode, which would confirm the merger hypothesis, but I did a reanalysis and it turned out to be an alias, caused by the observation times themselves. I decided that spectroscopy would be the best way to circumvent the limits of time-series analysis and test the hypothesis. I'm now the primary author and Co-I on a VLT/ESPRESSO proposal with Dr. Bogumił Pilecki (PI) to probe the system's merger history. 

Before that, I coordinated a multi-continent Slooh campaign with citizen scientists to determine the rotation period of main-belt asteroid 7605 Cindygraber. We had some difficulties with aliasing due to the 11.96-hour period, so I built a scheduler to automate the process of finding an observatory that would fill the phase gap. That was still unsatisfying, because it didn't solve the problem that got us there in the first place: our strategy was just 'observe whichever asteroid we need more data for.' So, I decided to find an empirical method to optimize the timing of observations. A few months later, I'm the sole author of an [entropy-guided cadence optimization framework](https://henryzimmerman.net/astcadence) that I presented at AAS 248.

The site documents all of the astronomy alongside my writing in history, philosophy, literature, and economics that gave me the analytical tools I need.

The homepage is a Canvas renderer of the Yale Bright Star Catalog with five featured objects plotted at their live sky positions: [CEP-1347](https://henryzimmerman.net/cep1347), [U Sgr](https://henryzimmerman.net/U_Sgr_abs), 7605 Cindygraber, 19243 Bunting, and 5745. Asteroid markers are 2-body propagated from astorb elements to the current date, so they drift across the sky as the page is revisited. Clicking any featured star opens a research card or links to SIMBAD. Background sky cutouts come from CDS hips2fits (DSS2) for stars and ATLAS via CATCH for asteroids; shape models from DAMIT.

Below the star field is a [live simulation](https://henryzimmerman.net/#cepheid-sim) of CEP-1347 built from real Fourier fits to six years of OGLE photometry and the [Pilecki+ (2022)](https://doi.org/10.3847/2041-8213/ac9fcc) radial velocity solution. Three mode buttons isolate the orbital motion (58.85 d), the first-overtone pulsation (0.690 d), or run both at their true physical frequency ratio. Real-time mode blinks at 4.3 cycles per second and ships with a photosensitivity warning. The HUD shows V-band magnitude, effective temperature, Cepheid radius, and orbital phase, all computed frame-by-frame in `cepheid-engine.js`. Orbital parameters (K1 = 28.5 km/s, K2 = 51.6 km/s, p = 1.27) are pulled directly from [Pilecki+ (2022)](https://doi.org/10.3847/2041-8213/ac9fcc) and [Espinoza-Arancibia and Pilecki (2025)](https://doi.org/10.3847/2041-8213/adb96b).

The [writing](https://henryzimmerman.net/#writing) section has ten essays and one podcast: a [refutation of Descartes' ontological argument](https://henryzimmerman.net/descartes), a [podcast series on theodicy](https://henryzimmerman.net/believingbelief) co-produced with Genie Han, an [Anzaldua critique](https://henryzimmerman.net/borderlands), a [visual poem responding to a Kay Walkingstick diptych](https://henryzimmerman.net/calls), a [history of the 1755 Lisbon earthquake's media aftermath](https://henryzimmerman.net/lisbon), a [paper on Afonso I of Kongo](https://henryzimmerman.net/afonso), a Phillipian opinion piece scrutinizing in-class writing assessments, two Revere articles on the AfD and Greenland, an [analysis of Japan's labor policy](https://henryzimmerman.net/japan), and a [piece on Siqueiros' Muerte al Invasor](https://henryzimmerman.net/muralism). There's also a [highlights](https://henryzimmerman.net/#highlights) section (Brace Center fellowship finalist, varsity cross country captain, Writing Center tutor, co-president of the Andover Economics Society, the Webster Award) and a [bookshelf](https://henryzimmerman.net/#bookshelf) of my all-time favorites.

The site is plain HTML, CSS, and JavaScript with no framework or build step. `main.js` drives the homepage star canvas and the featured-object modals, `cepheid-engine.js` runs the pulsation simulator, and `writing-nav.js` holds the prev/next sequence for every essay. CSS is split into three files: `critical.css` is inlined in `index.html` for first paint, `deferred.css` loads async, and `subpages.css` covers the article pages. A service worker in `sw.js` caches static assets with background revalidation and serves HTML network-first. Data files in `data/` hold the Yale Bright Star Catalog in custom binary, the OGLE photometry for CEP-1347, and a subset of my cadence optimization corpus. These decisions are made to ensure the optimal user experience alongside  maintainability. The site is deployed from `main` via GitHub Pages to [`www.henryzimmerman.net`](https://henryzimmerman.net).
