Asteroid Photometry · Cadence Optimization

Entropy-Guided Intra-Night Cadence

The demonstration uses asteroid 5745, a 4.09-hour rotator from the filtered ALCDEF-LCDB corpus I built. I picked it because the gap between uniform and optimized sampling is largest on this one: uniform 8-observation sampling fails to recover the true period in 100% of cross-validation seeds, while the optimized 8-observation cadence succeeds in 100%. The phase-folded V-band lightcurve is a 4-term Fourier model with a strongly bimodal shape (A2/A1 ≈ 13). The demo samples that model at eight, nine, or ten times within an 8-hour night, adds Gaussian noise at σ = 0.04 mag, and compares naïve uniform spacing with the cadence selected by CMA-ES optimization on the short-period bin.

For 5745, the two schedules differ visibly in which peak the periodogram favors, not just in the residual structure around the winning peak. Under uniform sampling the highest peak frequently lands at an alias of the true rotation (P ≈ 3.49 h or 4.91 h, depending on noise realization) or at another competing peak; under the optimized cadence the true peak at 4.087 h dominates the periodogram in every seed. On the 57-asteroid short-period holdout set (P ∈ [2.2, 8] h, never seen during training), the same procedure reduces the alias rate from AR = 0.69 to 0.59 at N = 8, 0.63 to 0.52 at N = 9, and 0.57 to 0.51 at N = 10. The N = 8 and N = 9 gains are the largest in the sweep (Δ ≈ 0.10); by N = 10 the marginal improvement narrows toward Δ ≈ 0.06. The corpus-level numbers above are averages: for asteroids like 5745, the optimized cadence's gain is total; for already easy or prohibitively difficult targets, it is negligible.

Schedules
Uniform
Even spacing across the 8-hour night, one observation every 8 / (N − 1) hours. The reference baseline. For N = 8 this produces a strong sidelobe at P ≈ 1.64 h and a secondary forest around 5.7 h, both visible in the periodogram.
Optimized
Intra-night positions selected by Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (Hansen & Ostermeier 2001), with the Shannon entropy of the period posterior as the loss and held-out asteroids for cross-validation. The N = 8 short-period winner: 0, 0.62, 1.39, 2.71, 3.83, 6.01, 6.85, 8.00 h.
Methods Notes

The notes below describe the demonstration's implementation choices and how they relate to the full pipeline.

The period axis shown in the demonstration is the same blind 2nd-order Fourier (FALC) periodogram used by the production pipeline: at each trial period the data is least-squares fit by m(t) = m0 + a1cos(2π t/P) + b1sin(2π t/P) + a2cos(4π t/P) + b2sin(4π t/P), and the displayed quantity is variance explained on [0, 1]. The trial-period grid runs from 2.2 h (the asteroid spin barrier; faster rotators are tidally disrupted) to 6.4 h, matching the production fmax = 24/2.2 cyc/d cutoff that excludes sub-spin-barrier half-period aliases. The displayed H is the Shannon entropy of the local maxima of the periodogram above a power floor of 0.30, weighted by exp(power / Tp) with Tp = 0.085. H = 0 means one dominant peak; higher values indicate spreading mass across multiple candidate periods. The full pipeline's optimization loss is the entropy of the Boltzmann posterior p(f) ∝ exp(−RSS(f) / T*) under the same Fourier fit, with T* = 2σ² fixed analytically: at this value the pseudo-posterior coincides with the exact Gaussian posterior for the assumed noise σ, and a separate τ sweep validates that ρ(H, AR) is flat across a broad band containing T*. The CMA-ES schedules embedded above are the actual winners selected by 5-fold cross-validation on a 248-asteroid training set, screened against nine baseline cadence families; the noise realization is fixed at σ = 0.04 mag (the same level used by the production cross-validation pipeline), and the morph between two schedules at fixed N is an index-matched linear interpolation purely for animation.
